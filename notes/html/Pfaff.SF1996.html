Steven Pfaff. 1996. "Collective Identity and Informal Groups in Revolutionary Mobilization: East Germany in 1989." Social Forces 75 (1): 91-118.

  1. Introduction
    • How did popular protest arise and what accounts for the seemingly spontaneous nature of the revolution? How could an atomized population foster enough solidarity to face the risk of state retribution? What are the factors that impelled social action?
    • Prior models fail to take account of value expectancy.
      • Do actors arrive at matters of personal value and calculate their expectations on the basis of individual experience alone? Can identities be considered fixed at the moment of movement participation or are they dynamic constructs? How did actors arrive at collective grievances, at the decision to risk their lives and welfare?
    • Viewing the experience of belonging to social groups as crucial to the construction of collective dientities, solidarities, and grievance frames that make risky protest action possible.
    • Revolution was facilitated by collective identities and social solidarities that arose in the private sphere of trusted friends and associates.
  2. Social movement theory and the East German Revolution
    • Need to move away from sheerly macrosocial models and suggest that prevailing theories of SMs be changed to include loosely structured and network-based collective action in authoritarian contexts.
    • Need to understand values and expectations of social actors -- they are not determined by political structures or political opportunities.
    • Vanguard groups had little power to spearhead mobilizations. Furthermore, protest started in Leipzig and not Berlin.
    • Both new social movement and resource mobilization theory cannot address authoritarian and non-Western societies.
    • Collective identities vis-a-vis Gould (1995) may be crucial factors.
    • Need to examine construction of collective identities and operation of social networks in this authoritarian context.
  3. Identity, solidarity, and the revolt against Communism
    • How collective identities are constructed and how actors are mobilized to revolt in a highly repressive society
    • Instead of seeing identities as constructed privately and prior to participation ("mobilization potential") identity should be seen as a dynamic construct drawn from collective narratives that can be transformed into movement participation itself.
    • Identities are situation within communities of shared beliefs, values, and ideologies that can precede formal organization.
    • It is on this bases that loyalty and trust are reached.
    • Sense of community fostered in small groups that are large enough to have an impact on the collective.
    • Social networks become potent mobilizers of collective action when they manage to activate a community's identities, grievances, and values in the context of a rapidly shifting political structure.
    • Networks nurture collective identities and solidarity, provide informal organization and contacts, and supply information otherwise unavailable to individuals.
    • Reason that revolution was surprising was that there's a deep contrast between public and private life in authoritarian societies.
    • Drawing on "hidden transcripts" (Scott 1990) of dissent - dissent and informal organization as socially embedded and opaque to authorities.
  4. Political opportunity and popular protest: an analysis of "Leipzig autumn"
    • Political structures and opportunity alone was important, but it was not wholly determinative.
    • Three key social elements: expectations of solidarity; framing of grievances based on widespread belief in social justice; and construction of an oppositional identity by protesters through the use of the concept of das Volk ("the People")
    • expectations of solidarity - Given the pervasiveness of state police, this meant a retreat to trusted, like-minded friends. Grievances were better expressed in the context of collectives. Individual nonparticipation would be suffereing unacceptable damage to an identity in which much is invested (Calhoun 1991).
    • social injustice - the values that the regime stood for could be used against it for its failure to extend political liberty and benefits of socialism equally.
    • formation of a protest identity - emblematic idea of the Volk, connecting the idea of German nationalism with socialism and democracy.
