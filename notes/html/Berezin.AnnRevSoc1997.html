Berezin, Mabel. 1997. "Politics and Culture: A Less Fissured Terrain." ARS 23.

  1. Mapping the field
    • Four subareas that have emerged as significant for the study of politics and culture: political culture, institutions, political communication and meaning, and collective action.
  2. Political culture versus politics and culture
    • There is a distinction between "politics and culture" and "political culture."
      • The former suggests that each term constitutes an autonomous realm.
      • The latter is the matrix of meanings embodied in expressive symbols, practices, and beliefs that constitute ordinary politics in a bounded collectivity. Assumption is that attitudes determine political practices.
    • Focus of political culture has turned to look at civil society and democratization.
    • Theories of civil society - civil society as the social space in which democractic polity is enacted.
      • According to Habermas, what separates democratic practices from what has come before is existence of a separate sphere that is neither state nor church, where individuals form publics and debate political ideas and practices.
    • Democracy and culture - Putnam, Tocqueville, etc.
  3. States and institutions
    • Interest in culture in defining state formation.
    • Looking into the cultural side of institutionalism.
  4. Political communication and meaning
    • Cultural practices provide empirical focus for analysis of the dissemination of knowledge about politics.
    • How do particular audiences receive political information?
    • Studying ritual and symbolic processes.
  5. Collective action
    • Cultural turn in study of contentious politics.
