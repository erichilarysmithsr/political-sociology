John McCarthy and Mayer Zald. 1973. "The Trend of Social Movements in America: Professionalization and Resource Mobilization."

Pam Oliver's intro

(from http://ssc.wisc.edu/~oliver/SOC924/Assignments/OrgsResources.htm)

This is the original. It is more subtle than you would think from citations to it. Thirty years later, it is an interesting historical retrospective. The "parts" are arbitrarily broken, but there are different themes in each section. It is exploring reasons for the intense movement mobilization of the 1960s. Part 1 reviews and dismisses a variety of explanations focuses on a "participation revolution" and concludes that the key is more students with discretionary time. Part 2 is most linked to issues of organization, with a lot of emphasis on money for movements, professional activists and movement careers. There is also a short section on the increasing use of mass media by movements. Part 3 continues the professionalization theme and asks whether movements will be shaped by the interests of those with money.

Article

  1. Introduction
    • "Resource mobilization" emphasizes the resources, beyond membership consciousness and manpower, that may become available to potential movements organizations.
    • Stresses structural conditions that faciliate the expression of grievances.
  2. Introduction [their label]
    • Has there actually been increased political participation during the 1960s?
    • If so, what are the structural causes of the rise?
    • What implications do changes in participation have concerning the assumptions and logic of accepted theories of social movements?
    • Assumption is that social movement orgs are dependent on their members for movement operation.
    • RM raises two questions: have American SMOs been as typically dependent upon members as "hearts and minds of the people" approach suggests? Do ebbs and flows of SMO activity over time directly reflect changing rates of socio-political participation within the population?
    • Wouldn't we expect decline of SM activity with increased per capita income? [grievance model]
    • What can be said of long-range trends? Shaped by particular historical moment? Or have their been structural changes?
    • They argue that functions of SMOs that have traditionally been run by members have been taken over by paid functionaries.
  3. Everyone a participant?
    • One argument is that there is more participation because society has become more middle class. Formal education as showing relationship to political participation.
    • No huge increase in participation as it comes to voting, nor political clubs, campaigns, political meetings.
  4. Components of participation: social structure and individual opportunities
    • Not just education. Need to consider costs of participation - participation requires some combination of money, discretionary time, and energy. These are unequally distributed throughout the class structure, and also ought to bear upon motivation and cognition.
    • Affluence and leisure - upper middle class is more likely to work longer hours and have less discretionary time. Increased affluence leads to an increase in maintenance of possessions. So middle class is not full of people with large amounts of discretionary time.
    • Organizational involvement and social status - Higher participation in middle classes may be due to increased money for organizational memberships. Evidence that people with more income give more to organizations that affect them less personally, with immediate benefits.
    • Student involvement - students can increasing rearrange their schedules to fit the needs to socio-political action.
    • Discretionary time and the professionals - Professionals can generally rearrange their schedules to fit in social movement activity.
    • Transitory teams - Existence of discretionary time and increasing incomes of sympathizers make it possible gor groups to be mobilized at relatively low cost for periods of time. Relative coordination costs have gone down as discretionary income and time have increased.
  5. Institutional funding and career supports
    • 1960s became a time in which institutional support for SMOs became available and careers in movements were viable. Also organizations not traditionally thought to be SM supporters were giving more and more support for them.
    • Church and foundation support - Growth of assets in foundations has been source for SM support. More leadership and funding support from churches.
    • Government funding of social movements - Government funding of organizations [like Vista? Could this be called an SMO?]; indirect fudning through grants and consultation with citizen groups.
    • Managerial and staff personnel - We see emergence of staffing of SMOs, part-time or for life careers.
      • Part-time participation - donation of staff time from firms and corporations. Participation as possibly being a criterion for hiring.
      • Temporary full-time positions - Vista and the Peace Corps. These serve as a training ground for those involved who have grievances.
      • Full-time careers - Growing institutionalization of dissent. Long-term positions in social movement organizations with stable incomes.
  6. The classical model and modern social movements
    • Classical model of social movements:
      1. analysis of class, category, or group of individuals with a common grievance or who are subject to common constraints. But this does not determine the birth of a social movement
      2. communication among the members of a group as crucial
      3. enviromental factors impinging on the group.
      4. if communication is effective, then concerted action.
      5. in early stages, possible ill-organized responses.
      6. after leadership emerges we find action.
      7. emergent leaders confront common problems of the group, helps them define and devise explanations of their occurrence (ideology)
      8. What needs to be brought in is (8) membership or mass base provides resources that allow movement to survive and carry out its program.
      9. Size and intensity of SMOs reflects existence of grievances.
      10. Once problems are solved, movement may disappear, or transformed and institutionalized.
      11. Leadership of the movement must be sensitive to membership as well.
    • Old model is not great, can be evidenced by leadership acting independently of membership.
    • The declining functions of membership base - modern movements can find resources outside of self-interested memberships concerned with grievances.
    • The separation of funding and leaders from the base - Financial support for a movement being totally separate from its presumed benefactors.
    • Mass media and movement organizations - Ability of groups to manipulate images of social problems with greater impact. Could just be appearing to be large but membership only existing on paper.
    • Growing trend to inclusive organizations - Larger pool for potential supporters with television. Relying less on any single base of support.
    • Professional social movements - Professional social movements rising from change in funding patterns.
      • Characterized by leadership that devotes full time to the movement.
      • Small or nonexistent membership base
      • Attempts to impact image of "speaking for a potential constituency"
      • Attempts to influence policy towards that constituency.
      • Definition of grievance will probably grow to expand and to meet funds and support personnel avaiable. Possibility of those that create grievances.
    • Stability and change in the new careers and organizations
      • The new careers - One feature of these organizations is that their funding is unstable. Possibility is that leaders of these organizations shift across different networks of orgs, foundations, and agencies. Accompanying it is a literature for these careers.
      • Product diversification change in movement organizations - SMOs that take on broader issues rather than single issues, have more of a chance of survival.
  7. Professional social movements in modern America: does the piper call the tune?
    • Social control and social movement analysis - how do elites attempt to exercise control over professional SMOs?
    • One way elites can control movements is by controlling funds for professional organizations.
      • Most institutionally-backed SMOs are reformist rather than radical for this reason. Directing dissent into legitimate channels.
    • Another way elites try to manage movements is to minimize their scope and quantity.
