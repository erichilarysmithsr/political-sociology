Karl-Dieter Opp and Christiane Gern. 1993. "Dissident Groups, Personal Networks, and Spontaneous Cooperation: The East German Revolution of 1989." ASR 58.

  1. Introduction
    • Networks are important, but conditions of communication under democracies are not the same under authoritarian states.
    • Protests on October 9, 1989 were not planned. They were spontaneous.
    • Need to deal with roles of organized groups and interpersonal ties in emergence of protests in a context of extensive state repression.
    • Goal is to specify general specification of factors that launch political protest in authoritarian regimes.
  2. Theory
    1. Basic model
      • Broad incentives within authoritarian settings that promote participation on a micro-level.
      • RCT model with four types of incentives: public goods motivation, moral incentives, social incentives,a nd repression.
      • Macro-factors that affect incentives
      • Social context (groups, networks, institutional incentives)
      • Political events, such as liberalization of Eastern Europe.
    2. Incentives to participate
      • Dissatisfaction with provision of public goods.
      • Moral incentives
      • Social incentives such as encouragement by others to participate
      • Repression deferring people from protesting
    3. The social context: social groups and personal networks
      • Groups
      • Voluntary associations as being controled by the regime.
      • Some groups emerged in early 80s that took a critical stance towards the political system.
      • Groups are important because of direct attempts at mobilization and because they become reference groups for other citizens.
      • Groups may also have organized demonstrations or other joint political actions.
      • Group integration furnishes individuals with a communications network, values and symbols, a tradition of participation, and an authority structure.
      • They expect zero/negative correlation between group membersip in conventional political groups and participation in protests.
      • Alternative hypothesis - any group has the potential for mobilization.
      • Personal networks
      • Networks are micro-contexts for mobilizing citizens.
      • Communication as largely constrained by the regime. 13% had phones.
      • Ways of establishing independent public expression emerge - subtle signals of political views may be exchanged. Politically homogeneous networks may emerge slowly.
      • Assuming important networks for mobilization consist of co-workers and friends.
    4. Political events - liberalization. [umm he needs to expand on this]
    5. Coordinating mechanisms; spontaneous cooperation
      • Positive incentives rose, but costs of protesting were still high.
      • Classic coordiantion problem. Institution of Monday prayers shaped this problem [omg direct connection between January 25 and January 28]
  3. Research design
    1. Sample
      • representative survey that focused on 1989. Interviews.
      • Concerns - proportion reporting participation as discrepant from news report estimates.
    2. Measurement
      • Possibility that people are distorting their memories of the revolution. But data were generally considered reliable.
      • Questionnaire starting with "what comes to mind when you think of period of revolution?"
      • Two sorts of bias: outcome effect - tendency for respondents to adjst their cognition of an event to its outcome. hero effect - refers to tendency for citizens to overstate their roles in the revolution, particularly their participation and the risks.
      • Outcome effect would lead to overstatement of respondents' perceived personal influence.
      • Hero effect would lead participants to overstate the possibility of repression. But not relevant since probably had truth to it.
      • Dependent variable as participation in demonstrations before and on October 9, 1989 -- susceptible to hero effect. To avoid this, respondents' reports on participation were part of a self-administered questionnaire that was handed to the interviewer in a clsoed envelope.
      • Interviewers were asked to evaluate each interviewee.
      • 19 citizens were selected for unstructured interviews.
  4. Variables
    • participation; public goods motivation; personal influence - asking about particular events; moral incentives; expected rewards from reference persons; group encouragement of protest - asked whether group encouraged participation in protests; probability of repression; severity of repression; experienced repression;
  5. Results
    • Model 1 (incentives) - Public goods motivation as the most important variable. Fear of repression as higher, but experienced repression as sig in model 1. Possible indirect radicalization effect.
    • Indirect efforts by oppoisition groups to mobilize depends on the extent to which groups are known. No evidence that opposition groups mobilized the population directly or were widely accepted reference groups b/c not a lot of ICT use.
    • Group membership - membership in opposition group as correlating with group encouragement. Also sports and church membership. Critical friends as correlating with public goods and expected rewards motivation.
    • Model 2 - expected effects for groups, but coefficients are small.
    • Model 3 throws membership and incentives into the whole mix. Group membership doesn't seem to explain much variance.
    • Measuring ties at the workplace and friends - personal networks as favorable context for mobilization.
      • Critical friends as most important
    • Problem of coordination as easy -- choosing Karl Marx Platz during Monday prayers.
  6. Discussion
    • Public goods motivation and critical friends as main explanatory variables.
