Doug McAdam, Sidney Tarrow, Charles Tilly. 2001. Dynamics of Contention.

Ch. 1 - What are they yelling about?

  1. Contentious politics
    • Contentious politics as showing how different forms of contentious - SM, revolutions, etc - result from similar mechanisms and processes.
    • Definition: episodic, public, collective interaction among makers of claims and their objects when (a) at least one government is a claimant, an object of claims, or a party to the claims and (b) the claims would, if realized, affect the interestsoof at least one of the claimants
      • This definition attempts to unite what are similar instances of contention due to shared mechanisms and processes.
      • Also attempts to remove the boundary between institutionalized and noninstitutionalized politics. Many instances of noninstitutional contention emerge from institutional contention.
  2. Contained and transgressive contention
    • Contained - cases in which all parties are previously established actors employing well-established means of claim making.
    • Transgressive - at least some parties to the conflict are newly self-identified politics actors and/or at least some parties employ innovative collective action. This book mostly focuses on this.
  3. What's news?
    • Identiyfing similarities and differences, pathways and trajectories across a wide range of contentious politics.
  4. From polity model to dynamics of contention
    • How to identify actors in contentious politics, their claims, the objects of those claims, and responses to claim making.
    • Two simplifications:
      • Polity model includes agents of government, polity members, challengers, subjects, and outside political actors.
      • Political actors as sets of individuals and relations among persons whose internal organization and connections with other political actors maintain substaintial continuity in time and space.
    • Moving to dynamic analysis
      • Social processes as sequences and combinations of causal mechanisms.
      • To explain contentious politics is to identify recurrent causal mechanisms, the ways they combine, in what sequences they recur, adn why different combinations and sequences, starting from different initial conditions, produce varying effects on the large scale.
  5. Classic social movement agenda
    • Attention of the social movement scholars on political opportunities, mobilizing structures, collective action frames, and repertoires of contention (the culturally encoded ways in which people interact in contentious politics)
    • Interaction between these served as the generic rubric for SM studies. However this worked less when it came to nondemocratic states and highlighted dynamic movements.
  6. Intellectual resources
    • Structuralist, rationalist, phenomoenological (individual, identity), cultural (structural and ideational)
    • Relational - thinking about interpersonal networks, interpersonal communication, and various forms of continuous negotiation as figuring centrally in the dyanmics of contention.
  7. Causal mechanisms, causal processes, contentious episodes
    • mechanisms - a delimited class of events that alter relations among specified sets of elements in identical or closely similar ways over a variety of situations.
    • processes - regular sequences of such mechanisms that produce similar transformations of those elements
    • episodes - continous streams of contention incluing collective claims making that bears on other parties' interests
    • environmental, cognitive, and relational mechanisms (akin to how Tilly (2003) describes them.
    • episodes as composed of several different processes.
    • task, then, is the following:
      • identifying contentious episodes or families of contentious episodes having some problematic feature
      • locating the processes within them that constitute or produce the problematic feature
      • searching out the crucial causal mechanisms within those processes.
    • How to delineate an episode? Depends on observers' lenses, how they name it.
    • Mechanisms and processes as workhorses of explanation, while episodes are that of description.
  8. Mobilization, Actors, Trajectories
    • mobilization - explain how people who are not making contentious claims begin to do so.
    • actors - explain what sorts of actors engage in contentioun, what identities they assume, and what forms of interaction they produce.
    • trajectories - explaining the course and transofmraiton of contention.
