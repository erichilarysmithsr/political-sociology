Gould, Roger V. 1993. "Collective action and network structure." ASR 58(2)

  1. Introduction
    • Importance of networks and interdependent decisions in protest.
    • In this article, presenting a formulation of the link between individual participation and collective efficacy predicated on different assumptions: norms of fairness that encourage individuals to match contributions of others, and the desire to avoid making contributions that will be wasted.
    • Then developing a model that relates individual decisions about participation in collective action to networks of social ties that inform actors about the behavior of others.
      • Depends strong on the structural position of "volunteers" - when they are centrally located, dense networks offer dimmer prospects for mobilization than sparser networks.
      • Group size has variable effects, depending on density.
      • Tendencies toward intransitivity enhance mobilization by contributing to connectedness in relatively sparse networks.
  2. Theory
    • Individuals are not utility maximizers, because fairness norms play a central part in the production of collective goods.
    • At the same time, however, decisions to contribute are informed by expectations of individual efficacy.
    • Boundary conditions
      • Efficacy - individuals must perceive a difference in the potential impact of their own efforts based on the efforts of others.
      • Initial contributions must represent sunk costs - individuals cannot retract contributions if responses are not forthcoming.
      • Actors must perceive themselves as members of an identifiable collectivity.
